# Article Blueprint

## The Five Beats

### 1. The Hook (1–3 sentences)

Open with a concrete, specific claim, observation, or tension — not background. Lead with the insight or the contradiction that made you want to write the piece. A reader decides in the first paragraph whether to keep going. Give them a reason.

Avoid: "Recently I was reading X and it made me think about Y, which is interesting because…"
Instead: "Y is broken, and X explains exactly why."

### 2. The Stakes (1–2 short paragraphs)

Now you earn the right to give context — but only enough to answer "why should I care?" Frame the problem, gap, or tension your piece addresses. Be specific about who this matters to and what's at risk. This is where you establish that you're not just observing, you're arguing something.

### 3. The Argument (3–5 sections — the bulk of the piece)

Each section makes one distinct point that advances your central claim. Every section must do new work. If a paragraph restates what the previous one said in different words, cut it or merge it.

For each point, follow a rhythm: claim → evidence or example → implication. Don't just show *what* — show *so what*. Make your sharpest observations your topic sentences, not asides.

### 4. The Turn (1 paragraph)

Acknowledge the strongest counterargument, the limitation of your thesis, or the nuance that complicates things. This is what separates thought leadership from hot takes. It builds credibility and shows you've thought past the obvious.

### 5. The Close (2–4 sentences)

End with a specific, declarative statement — not a vague gesture toward the future. No "only time will tell" or "it will be interesting to see." Land on a concrete takeaway, a provocation, or a reframe of your opening that shows the reader has arrived somewhere new.

---

## Two Rules That Matter More Than Structure

**Know your one-sentence thesis before you write.** If you can't state it in a single sentence, you don't have an article yet — you have a topic.

**Every paragraph must pass the delete test.** If you remove it and the piece still makes sense, it wasn't earning its place.

## What's Working (keep doing this)

- **Your curiosity is your best asset.** The articles where you chase a question to its logical conclusion — Halo physics, S3 bucket limits, HPC with Step Functions — are by far your strongest. The math-meets-engineering voice is distinctive and engaging. Lean into this harder.
- **Real-world stories hit.** The AWS intern who nearly consumed an entire region's compute (Lambda recursion), the VMware PATH ghost (cleaning up old paths), the Up banking disruption — these anecdotes give your technical points weight. You have good instincts for when a story illustrates a point better than an explanation.
- **You're good at building complexity gradually.** The S3 article walks from a simple question through increasingly sophisticated math without losing the reader. The contact tracing piece builds from architecture diagram to working app. When you trust this instinct, it works.
- **Your conversational asides add personality.** "I know. I know. I know." in the flags piece, "have you seen this website?" as self-deprecation — these moments make your voice distinctive. Don't over-polish them out.

## Patterns to Fix (drawn from all 14 articles)

- **Front-load insights.** Don't warm up — start sharp. Almost every article opens with context-setting instead of a claim. The Halo piece opens with "My all time favorite video game series is the halo franchise" — that's a diary entry, not a hook. The Lambda recursion piece opens with "I was scrolling on LinkedIn the other day" — burying the actual point. The S3 piece is one of the few that leads with tension ("I'm here to tell you that is not entirely the truth"). Do more of that.
- **Land the ending.** Nearly every piece trails off. The flags article ends with "I guess we'll have to wait for GPT-5" — a shrug, not a conclusion. The Halo piece ends with a question ("could you drive a tank on a spinning ring?") that never gets answered. The Up piece closes with "Don't stop innovating" — generic advice that could end any business article. Write your last sentence with the same energy as your best analytical paragraph.
- **Flip the ratio.** The flags article spends ~80% on ChatGPT transcripts and ~20% on analysis. The contact tracing article is mostly tutorial with the "why this matters" buried. The Up article has strong analytical moments but they're sandwiched between long setup paragraphs. Your insight-to-setup ratio should be at least 60/40 in favor of insight.
- **Promote your best ideas.** Your sharpest observations keep ending up in block quotes or parenthetical asides. In the flags piece, the real insight — "these AIs are really good at getting to 80% of what an expert can do, but the final 20% is still out of reach" — arrives in the last three paragraphs after 400+ lines of ChatGPT output. That should be your opening thesis.
- **Cut the restating.** Multiple articles repeat a point in slightly different words across consecutive sentences. Example from the Up piece: "Earning trust with consumers is an incredibly hard endeavour" followed by advice to "over-communicate" followed by "that's exactly what Up does" — three sentences making one point. Pick the strongest version and delete the others.
- **One pass for commas.** Comma splices appear in almost every article. "This seems pretty fast, and it is, for context at the equator the earth spins at..." — that's three independent clauses chained together. Read the final draft once looking only for these.
- **Proofread for credibility.** Consistent typos across the portfolio: "devlopers," "megastuctures," "explainations," "avaliable," "buisness," "farmiliar," "sence," "methdology," "Gerneral," "symbolysm," "prvilaged." On technical topics especially, these erode authority. Run a spell checker as a final step.
- **Tutorials need a thesis too.** The Holopin, Revue, and Next.js migration pieces read as step-by-step walkthroughs with no argument. Even a tutorial benefits from a point of view. The Next.js piece gets close at the end ("I would say no, the effort isn't worth it") — that should be the opening, not the conclusion.
- **Be careful with "interesting."** The word appears dozens of times across your articles as a filler adjective. "It's been interesting to refactor," "Another interesting fact," "What people might find really interesting here." Each time, it's doing no work. Replace it with what specifically makes the thing worth noting, or cut it.

## Article Type Cheat Sheets

### For exploratory/math pieces (your strongest type)
Open with the question. State what you expected vs. what you found. Walk through the reasoning. Close with the implications or the next question.

Best examples to study from your own work: S3 bucket limits, Halo physics.

### For opinion/thought leadership pieces
Open with your claim. Support it with evidence and examples. Acknowledge the counterargument. Close with a specific takeaway.

Best example to study from your own work: Lambda recursion (closest to this structure), Up banking review (strong material, needs tighter structure).

### For tutorials
Open with what the reader will be able to do and why it matters. Walk through the steps. Close with what you'd do differently or what to watch out for.

Best example to study from your own work: In-App Purchases in Electron (clearest problem statement of the tutorial pieces).
