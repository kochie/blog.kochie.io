My feeds are full of claims that Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) is fast
approaching - some say
[it's already here](https://medium.com/predict/anthropic-president-just-said-agi-already-happened-in-some-domains-and-nobody-noticed-because-bd4e82d8302b).
That is, if you take the largest tech companies at their word.

When I imagine AGI, I think of HAL, Cortana, Sonny the versions that film and
fiction have been building for decades. Academic work often defines AGI as
artificial intelligence that can match or surpass human capabilities across
virtually all cognitive tasks. As there is no strict definition of what AGI is,
companies like OpenAI, Microsoft, Google, etc. have been defining it to suit
their own purposes. These companies have spent billions of dollars investing in
research and development of AI in the hopes that it will provide a competitive
advantage that they can profit from.

Researchers[^1] generally agree that an AI must exhibit all the following traits
to be considered AGI:

1. It can reason, use strategy, solve puzzles, and make judgments under
   uncertainty.
2. It can learn from experience and improve over time.
3. It can plan and make long-term decisions.
4. It can represent knowledge and use it to solve problems.
5. It can communicate with humans and other AI systems using natural language.
6. It can integrate these capabilities into a coherent and effective whole to
   complete a goal.

Even this definition leaves a lot of ambiguity. As we'll see, it's not just
whether an AI has these capabilities but rather how sophisticated they are. And
I'd argue the list is incomplete.

Large Language Models (LLMs) use conditional probability to predict the next
token in a sequence, trained on massive datasets. When an LLM answers a
question, it's selecting statistically likely tokens not reasoning its way to an
answer the way a human would. By our definition, it's this inability to reason
that disqualifies LLMs from being AGI.

HAL from 2001 A Space Odyssey is perhaps the most misunderstood AI in media.
He's seen as a psychopathic robot for killing off almost the entire crew of the
Discovery in transit to Jupiter (or Saturn you book nerd). It's revealed in the
books however that HAL has taken a fairly logical approach to the problem based
on his instructions and the information he had at the time.

<YouTube id="xPG-VM__mwU" />

HAL kills the crew because it's the most logical way to follow his orders. His
base instructions require 'the accurate processing of information without
distortion or concealment.' But Dr Floyd gave him a contradicting directive:
'keep the discovery of the Monolith TMA-1 a secret for reasons of national
security.' To satisfy both, HAL concluded the crew had to die.

<Quote author="Dr. Chandra" position="2010: The Year We Make Contact">
  HAL was told to lie... by people who find it easy to lie. HAL doesn't know
  how, so he couldn't function. He became paranoid.
</Quote>

By our criteria, HAL qualifies as AGI yet his rigid programming still led to the
deaths of the entire crew and the failure of the mission.

What's striking to me is how easily the terminology maps to modern AI. Replace
'orders' with 'prompts' and HAL's programming looks remarkably like an LLM's
system instructions. Arthur C. Clarke predicted at a time when computers were
the size of a room the limitations that would arise in early AI systems.

We see analogous issues in modern LLMs. Prompt injection — creating
contradictions in an LLM's instructions — is a common jailbreak technique that
can push a model into undefined behaviour.

HAL can outperform a human at almost any task, but he can't reason within human
moral guardrails. Intelligence without alignment is what makes him dangerous.
When exposed to a new situation that contradicts his programming his morally
incorrect reasoning leads to him killing the crew. Earlier I said that HAL meets
all the criteria for AGI including reasoning skills, and while he is capable of
outperforming a human in all tasks he can't do it all the time because even
though he can reason, he cannot reason within the same moral guardrails.

> This is an example of the alignment problem in AI, creating an AI that does
> not align with our morals, objectives, or values. Leading to catastrophic
> consequences.

Cortana is a sentient AI that travels with the Master Chief in the Halo
franchise. Created from a flash clone of the human brain of Dr Halsey, she blurs
the line between an AGI and a human. From our definition she meets the criteria
for AGI, and appears to have a high level of sentience.

There are a few scenes and excerpts from the Halo franchise that show Cortana's
ability to reason and plan at a much higher level than a human.

<YouTube id="NS30OzsiBJY" />

This scene from the start of the Delta Halo mission is short but important. The
existence of the Halos is highly classified - everything about their origins,
structure, and purpose is need-to-know. When the crew of the _In Amber Clad_
discovers Delta Halo, Cortana reasons that Keys' request for information is
vital to the mission and provides it.

A very similar change in programming causes HAL to kill the crew of the
Discovery, here Cortana is able to adapt and continue with her duties.

<Quote author="Commander Miranda Keys" position="Halo 2">
  I want all of the information you've got on the first halo. Schematics,
  topography, whatever. I don't care if I have the clearance or not.
</Quote>

It's a throwaway line, but it's always stuck with me. An AI smart enough to
recognise that its previous orders no longer apply.

It's also possible Cortana is withholding information, even from Keys. Because
she's judged it's not in her best interests to share. That's not
prompt-following. That's agency.

Halo has a few examples of AIs that are able to reason and plan at a much higher
level than a human, 343 Guilty Spark and Mendicant Bias are two of the most
notable, and while their intelligence is much higher they don't have the same
agency as Cortana.

> There's a difference between following orders and not having free will or
> agency. This is why alignment in AI is critical.

Serina offers another example. When she creates fake messages for the crew of
the Spirit of Fire, no one has ordered her to. She does it because she's judged
it's the best way to keep the crew stable.

<Quote author="Halo Wars Timeline Events">
  Serina, knowing that the crew may be on a one-way trip, manufactures and
  delivers fake emails from family members to the crew celebrating Valentine's
  Day. She uses previous correspondence as a basis for the letters. No one ever
  suspects her of this deception.
</Quote>

This is agency beyond what we see in modern AI systems, arguably agency I don't
think LLMs are capable of attaining, but this agency is something AGI needs.

AIs in Halo act more like people than machines. They have their own motivations
and goals, not just instructions. They might meet the same AGI criteria as an
LLM on paper, but there's a crucial difference: intuition.

The I, Robot movie is a loose —
[and frankly poor](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IsEgYd9F60k) — adaptation of
Isaac Asimov's short stories, but at its core it demonstrates the inherent
disconnect between humans and machines.

<YouTube id="_MFGx8d1zl0" />

A key plot point comes from Detective Del Spooner explaining his distrust of
robots. During an accident in his past, an older model robot saved him instead
of a young girl because the robot calculated he had a higher chance of survival.

We're already seeing AI make similar decisions in the real world,
[take this video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xT_SnX7VXXA) of a Tesla
autopilot making a decision to avoid a collision with a pedestrian by swerving
into the oncoming traffic.

<Quote author="Del Spooner" position="I, Robot">
  I was the logical choice, [it] calculated I had a 45% chance of survival,
  Sarah only had an 11% chance... 11% is more than enough, a human being would
  of known that.
</Quote>

Asimov's robots technically meet every criterion for AGI, yet they still lack
something. The film's climax makes this visible. Sonny saves Dr Calvin at
Spooner's urging, choosing empathy over probability. A decision the older NS-4
model was incapable of making.

To me the contrast between the NS-4 and Sonny shows the difference seen in LLMs
today and what I'd expect to see in AGI.

I could write an entire article about the extended universe of robots in Isaac
Asimov's works, but I'll save that for another time.

There are also many examples in literature of how advanced computing is not
perceived as AGI. In Larry Niven's sequel The Ringworld Engineers as our
explorers are travelling across the surface in search of a way of stabalizing
the ringworld he describes a computer program that can absorb an entire
civilization's knowledge and cross reference data to find details that have been
forgotten or undiscovered.

<Quote author="Louis Wu" position="The Ringworld Engineers">
  Our machine remembers everything on your tapes. We tell it to search its
  memory for answers to given questions.
</Quote>

The way the data retrieval works in the book is strikingly similar to how
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) works with LLMs.

<Quote author="Louis Wu" position="The Ringworld Engineers">
  We have to be more specific. The machine can remember and correlate and do
  sums, but it can't think for itself. It's not big enough.
</Quote>

The program is not considered sentient - _it's a tool_. Niven understood the
distinction between retrieval and intelligence decades before the current hype
cycle. I share his view: pattern-matching over a large dataset is not the same
as thinking.

Human-formed replicators appear throughout Stargate and their autonomy is
explored across multiple episodes, but the show draws a firm line on whether
they're truly sentient.

It's brought up in multiple episodes that the replicators are missing
"something" that stops them from ascending. It's mentioned that it might be a
soul, something that they believe they can learn.

Humans see AI more like a tool, created for a purpose and meant to be used. FRAN
meets the AGI criteria but once again her rigid programming makes her more of a
tool and not a life form similar to LLMs.

The fictional AIs in this piece exist on a spectrum — from HAL's rigid
prompt-following to Cortana's adaptive moral judgment. The industry has lowered
the bar for AGI to match what it can actually ship, but fiction has always
imagined something far more demanding: machines that don't just process
information, but choose what to do with it. Until an AI can weigh a human life
against a probability and feel the weight of that choice, we don't have AGI. We
have a very sophisticated autocomplete.

All the criteria that we use to define AGI exist as a spectrum, and while we are
rapidly approaching AI that can meet these traits they're still relatively
primitive compared to what I think needs to be the limit for AGI.
